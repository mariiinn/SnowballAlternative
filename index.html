The sleep cycle

Our sleep cycle has four different stages that repeats about every 90 minutes through the night. That is three NREM (Non-rapid eye movement) stages and a REM (Rapid eye movement) stage. The first stage (N1) is the "dozing off" stage. Breathing rate slows, but the body isn’t fully relaxed yet. In the next stage, N2 sleep, the body temperature drops, muscles relax and breathing becomes regular. Brain waves change and eye movement stops. A person typically spends around half their sleep time in N2 sleep. N3 is also called deep sleep or delta sleep (SWS – short-wave sleep). The body relaxes even further. It is believed that N3 sleep is critical to restorative sleep, that is sleep that allows the body to restore health and growth. Then we enter REM sleep. Breathing becomes irregular, the heart beats faster and the eyes moves. At this time the body experiences temporary paralysis which means you wouldn't actually start running if you dream about running. Though there is possible to dream in earlier stages, the dreams accumulated in this stage are more vivid and common.

REM sleep

Around a quarter of the night is spent in REM sleep. A person spends nearly 600 hours dreaming each year during REM sleep. It is believed you can learn in your dreams, strengthen memory and creativity. In a busy world where we never have enough time, we want to exploit the hours spent in REM sleep to expand horizons.

Dream manipulation today
